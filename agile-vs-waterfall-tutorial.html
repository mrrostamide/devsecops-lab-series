Part 1 of 5 - Core Concepts

What are these two approaches?

Both methods manage projects from start to finish - but they think about planning, delivery, and change very differently.

Waterfall

Plan everything, then build.

Like constructing a building - you finalize the blueprint before laying a single brick. Each phase must finish before the next begins.

  • All requirements defined upfront
  • Strict sequential phases
  • Detailed documentation throughout
  • Changes are expensive and disruptive
  • Final product delivered at the end
Agile

Build small. Learn fast. Adapt.

Like cooking by tasting as you go - you deliver something working quickly, get real feedback, and improve it through short repeating cycles called sprints.

  • Requirements evolve based on feedback
  • Short sprints (1-4 weeks each)
  • Working software over heavy documentation
  • Change is expected and welcomed
  • Working features delivered continuously
A simple analogy: If you order a pizza, Waterfall means the chef finalizes the full menu, sources all ingredients, cooks everything at once, then serves you - exactly as planned. Agile means the chef brings you a small slice first, asks "more spice?", adjusts, brings another slice, and keeps improving until you're happy.

When to use each?

Choose Waterfall when...

  • Requirements are fixed and fully known from day one
  • Regulatory sign-offs require sequential phase completion
  • You are building physical infrastructure (bridges, hardware)
  • The client cannot be involved frequently during the project
  • Risk of change mid-project is very low

Choose Agile when...

  • Requirements are unclear or likely to evolve
  • Speed to market and early user feedback matter
  • The product is digital and can be iterated quickly
  • The team is cross-functional and collaborative
  • The client can participate in regular reviews
