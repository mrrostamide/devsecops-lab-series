CIA Triad

The three pillars of information security

Every security decision ultimately protects one or more of three properties. Together they define what it means for information to be secure.

  • Confidentiality - only authorized parties can read the data
  • Integrity - data is accurate and has not been tampered with
  • Availability - authorized users can access data when needed
  • Achieving all three simultaneously is the core challenge of security
  • Every attack violates at least one pillar - often more than one
Analogy
A hospital records room

A hospital file room needs: a lock so only authorized staff can enter (Confidentiality), tamper-evident folders so no one can alter records undetected (Integrity), and 24/7 access for emergency doctors (Availability). Removing any one of these creates a security failure.

The tension Maximizing one pillar can undermine another. Making data available to everyone reduces Confidentiality. Encrypting everything with a lost key protects Confidentiality but destroys Availability. Security is the art of balancing all three.
Non-Repudiation

Prove that a specific action occurred and who performed it

Non-repudiation ensures that a party cannot deny having performed an action. It combines proof of origin, proof of delivery, and an unforgeable audit trail - typically using asymmetric cryptography.

  • Digital signatures - only the private key holder could have signed
  • Tamper-evident audit logs - every action recorded and verifiable
  • PKI certificates - bind identities to cryptographic keys
  • Distinct from authentication - auth proves who you ARE; non-repudiation proves what you DID
  • Used in email (S/MIME), code signing, financial transactions
Analogy
A signed contract with a notary

When you sign a contract before a notary, you cannot later claim you never signed it. The notary's stamp, your signature, and the witness create non-repudiation. A digital signature plays all three roles - and is mathematically stronger than a handwritten one.

Problem it solves "I never sent that wire transfer order." / "Someone must have used my account." / "I deny ever accessing that file." Non-repudiation makes denial impossible when proof exists.
Compliance

Meeting external security obligations

Compliance means demonstrating that an organization meets a defined set of requirements - whether from law, regulation, contract, or industry standards. It is a minimum baseline, not a security guarantee.

  • Gap analysis - identify where current controls fall short
  • Control mapping - document which controls meet which requirements
  • Evidence collection - prove controls are operating effectively
  • Audit readiness - internal and external audit processes
  • Compliance does not equal security - a compliant organization can still be breached
Analogy
A driving test vs being a good driver

Passing a driving test proves you meet a minimum legal standard, not that you are an expert in all conditions. Passing a compliance audit proves minimum requirements at a point in time. A company can be fully compliant in January and breached in February.

The critical distinction Security is ongoing risk management. Compliance is a point-in-time snapshot against fixed criteria. Both matter, but confusing the two is dangerous.
Standards and Regulations

Frameworks that define security requirements

Security standards and regulations provide structured sets of controls and requirements. Regulations are legally mandatory. Standards are typically voluntary frameworks - but are often used to demonstrate compliance with regulations.

  • GDPR - EU regulation for personal data protection (mandatory)
  • HIPAA - US regulation for healthcare data (mandatory in scope)
  • ISO 27001 - international standard for security management (voluntary)
  • NIST CSF - US framework for cybersecurity risk management (voluntary)
  • PCI DSS - payment card industry standard (mandatory if processing cards)
Analogy
Building codes and architect certification

Building codes are regulations - legally required, enforced by inspectors. Architect certification is a standard - voluntary, but widely expected. Security regulations (GDPR, HIPAA) work like building codes. Security standards (ISO 27001, NIST CSF) work like professional certification.

Key rule Regulations impose legal obligations with penalties. Standards provide guidance and credibility. An organization may choose ISO 27001 voluntarily but has no choice about GDPR if it processes EU personal data.

Confidentiality

Only authorized parties can access the information

Confidentiality ensures that data is not disclosed to unauthorized individuals, processes, or systems - whether in storage, transit, or during processing.

  • AES-256 encryption of data at rest
  • TLS/HTTPS for data in transit
  • Role-based access control (RBAC)
  • Need-to-know data classification
  • Multi-factor authentication
⚠ Attacked by: Data exfiltration, eavesdropping, insider threats
Integrity

Data is accurate and has not been tampered with

Integrity ensures that information remains correct and complete, and that any unauthorized modification - accidental or deliberate - can be detected.

  • SHA-256 / SHA-3 cryptographic hashing
  • Digital signatures on files and messages
  • Checksum verification on downloads
  • Database transaction logs
  • Version control and change management
⚠ Attacked by: Man-in-the-middle, SQL injection, ransomware
Availability

Authorized users can access the system when they need it

Availability ensures that systems and data are accessible to authorized users on demand. Downtime - whether from attacks, failures, or misconfigurations - violates availability.

  • Redundant servers and failover systems
  • DDoS mitigation services (Cloudflare, AWS Shield)
  • Regular backup and disaster recovery testing
  • Load balancing across multiple zones
  • Defined Recovery Time Objectives (RTO)
⚠ Attacked by: DDoS attacks, ransomware, hardware failure

Key standards and regulations

ISO/IEC 27001

Voluntary standard

International standard for establishing, implementing, and maintaining an Information Security Management System (ISMS). Certifiable by accredited auditors.

GDPR

Mandatory regulation (EU)

General Data Protection Regulation. Applies to any organization processing EU personal data regardless of location. Fines up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework

Voluntary framework (US)

Five-function model: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. Widely adopted globally as a risk management reference.

PCI DSS

Mandatory (if processing cards)

Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard. Mandatory for any organization that stores, processes, or transmits payment card data.

HIPAA

Mandatory regulation (US)

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act. Protects patient health information (PHI) in the United States.

SOC 2

Voluntary audit report

Auditing framework based on five Trust Service Criteria: Security, Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, and Privacy. Common for SaaS companies.
